Submission to Defra 2026 Consultation

Three Standards, One Loophole

How Three Incompatible Testing Regimes Leave a Gap in UK Smoke Control Regulation

Submitted to: Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra)
Date: 17 March 2026
Classification: Technical Evidence / Statutory Consultation Response

Executive Summary

This report presents verified evidence that the UK's Smoke Control Area framework operates under three separate and incompatible emission testing regimes. These regimes use different pollutants, different measurement units, and different testing methodologies. The result is a regulatory structure in which the fuel and the appliance that burns it are tested under fundamentally different frameworks — and there is no published mechanism to reconcile them.

The three regimes are:

1. Authorised Fuels (BS 3841:1994): Tests the fuel in a standard open fire. Collects total particulate mass using an electrostatic precipitator or dilution tunnel. Reports results in grams per hour (g/h). Pass/fail threshold: 5 g/h. No limits for CO, NOx, or OGC. Only additional requirement: 2% sulphur content cap (since 2020).

2. Defra Exempt Appliances (PD 6434:1969): Tests the appliance. Pass/fail threshold: historically 5 g/h + 0.1g per 0.3 kW, reduced to 3 g/h for new certifications since January 2023 under the Environmental Improvement Plan. CO, VOC recorded but not pass/fail criteria. PM₂.₅ concentration not reported.

3. Ecodesign Stoves (EU 2015/1185, retained UK law): Tests the appliance. Limits: PM 40 mg/m³, CO 1,500 mg/m³, NOx 200 mg/m³, OGC 120 mg/m³ (all at 13% O₂). Uses heated filter method which captures filterable PM only. Ricardo (2022) found this method likely understates PM₂.₅.

The central finding: Regimes 1 and 2 report in g/h (emission rate). Regime 3 reports in mg/m³ (concentration). These units cannot be directly compared without knowing the flue gas volumetric flow rate under test conditions. No published government document reconciles them. It is currently impossible for any consumer, regulator, or researcher to directly compare the emission performance of an authorised fuel with the appliance designed to burn it.

Part 1

Three Testing Regimes — A Technical Comparison

1.1 Regime 1: Authorised Fuel Testing (BS 3841:1994)

To be authorised for sale in UK Smoke Control Areas, a manufactured solid fuel must meet two criteria: an average smoke emission of less than 5 grams per hour when tested to BS 3841, and sulphur content of less than 2% by dry weight. There is no PM₂.₅ concentration limit. No CO, NOx, or OGC limits. No minimum thermal efficiency requirement.

Ricardo Energy & Environment (August 2022), in a report commissioned by Defra (Evidence Review into Emission Assessments of Domestic House Stoves, Report ED16697100), noted that total PM measurement can be considered equivalent to PM₂.₅ because most emitted PM from residential solid fuel appliances falls within the PM₂.₅ fraction. The issue is not that PM₂.₅ particles escape uncaptured — the issue is that the result is reported only as total smoke mass in g/h, with no PM₂.₅-specific limit, and CO, NOx, and OGC are not measured at all.

1.2 Regime 2: Defra Exempt Appliance Testing (PD 6434:1969)

The test protocol is PD 6434:1969 with smoke measurement per BS 3841-2:1994. The HETAS Appliance Exemption Application Guidance (SCA01 Issue 2.0, October 2019) specifies: 5 tests at rated output plus 5 at minimum output. Only smoke emission (g/h) determines pass or fail. CO, VOC, and other measurements do not affect the exemption decision. PM₂.₅ concentration is not reported. NOx is not measured. Since January 2023, the policy limit for new certifications has been reduced to 3 g/h.

1.3 Regime 3: Ecodesign (EU 2015/1185)

Since 1 January 2022, all new solid fuel local space heaters must comply with Ecodesign requirements: PM 40 mg/m³, CO 1,500 mg/m³, NOx 200 mg/m³, OGC 120 mg/m³ (closed-front stoves at 13% O₂), and thermal efficiency ≥65%. The primary test method uses a heated filter, which captures filterable PM only — and Defra's own evidence review found this likely understates PM₂.₅.

Part 2

The Comparability Problem — and the Open Fireplace Loophole

The 2026 consultation proposes tighter limits for appliances. It does not propose any change to how fuels are tested or authorised. The fuel and the appliance continue to be assessed under incompatible frameworks. No mechanism exists to compare them.

The Open Fireplace Loophole: An authorised fuel may be burned in any appliance in a Smoke Control Area, including an open fireplace, because the fuel's authorised status provides the legal defence. No appliance approval is required. An open fireplace is 10–20% thermally efficient; an Ecodesign stove is 65%+ efficient. For every unit of useful heat, an open fireplace releases 4–8 times more combustion products — yet Smoke Control Area regulations permit this combination.

Part 3

Is Smokeless Fuel Actually Cleaner?

The University of Surrey and King's College London field study (Scientific Reports, July 2025) measured real indoor air quality in five UK homes during winter. Open fireplaces produced median indoor PM₂.₅ of 38.4 µg/m³ — approximately 7.7 times the WHO annual guideline of 5 µg/m³. Smokeless coal increased ultrafine particle exposure by a factor of 1.5 compared with seasoned wood when burned in the same type of appliance, and had the highest PM₂.₅/PM₁₀ ratio (0.62 ± 0.29) of all fuels tested.

The strongest argument is not that smokeless fuel is dirtier than wood in every scenario. The strongest argument is: (a) the current testing framework does not provide the data needed to make that comparison; (b) the open fireplace loophole permits the worst-case scenario; and (c) the 2026 consultation does not address either gap. All three are verifiable facts.

Part 4

What the 2026 Consultation Proposes — and What It Does Not

What Is Proposed

Part 1: Reduce smoke limit for new appliances from 3 g/h (reduced from 5 g/h in January 2023) to 1 g/h (+ 0.1g per 0.3 kW). Establish one testing regime. Introduce periodic retesting. 70% of stoves tested since 2018 already meet this. Estimated cumulative 1.08 kt PM₂.₅ abatement across 2027–2036 (1.9% of UK total PM₂.₅).

Part 2: Mandatory A–C emissions labelling with health statement. Health labelling for manufactured solid fuels.

Part 3: Increased penalties for non-compliance with 2020 Solid Fuels Regulations. Increased SCA fixed penalty notices.

What Is NOT Proposed

No update to BS 3841: Smokeless coal continues to be authorised under a test with no PM₂.₅ concentration limit and no CO, NOx, or OGC measurement.

No restriction on open fireplaces: The least efficient and most polluting appliance type remains unrestricted in SCAs for anyone burning an authorised fuel.

No unit harmonisation: The new 1 g/h limit uses g/h. Ecodesign uses mg/m³. No mechanism to compare them.

No update to PD 6434:1969: The 57-year-old protocol remains the basis for Smoke Control Area appliance exemption.

Part 5

The Health and Economic Context

UK air pollution is equivalent to 29,000–43,000 deaths annually among adults over 30, with an economic cost exceeding £20 billion per year (Defra 2026, citing COMEAP). Domestic burning accounts for 20.1% of UK PM₂.₅ (2023) — comparable to road transport at 20.5%. PM₂.₅ from domestic burning increased by 36% between 2009 and 2020. Up to 1,500 non-essential deaths per year are attributable to domestic burning specifically, with direct NHS costs of up to £54 million annually.

Ireland — Evidence That Fuel-Level Intervention Saves Lives: Dublin's 1990 ban on bituminous coal sales produced a 70% reduction in black smoke concentrations and a 5.7% decrease in non-trauma death rates. Respiratory deaths fell by 15.5%. Approximately 8,000 premature deaths have been averted since 1990. Ireland intervened at the fuel level. The UK's 2026 consultation proposes intervention only at the appliance level.

Part 6

Governance — Who Tests, Who Certifies, Who Advises

HETAS administers both the Defra exempt appliance scheme and the authorised fuel certification process. HETAS is the direct successor to DSFAAS, the testing arm of the solid fuel industry during the National Coal Board era. Revenue is derived from industry certification and registration fees. HETAS also produces and maintains the Defra Smoke Control Area website (smokecontrol.defra.gov.uk).

The Stove Industry Association (SIA, Companies House 06594851) and clearSkies Mark Ltd (Companies House 12311369) share the same registered address and directors. In November 2025, the Advertising Standards Authority upheld complaints against two SIA claims about stove emissions, finding that the data did not consistently demonstrate lower emissions across all key pollutants (ASA Ruling A25-1292745). All company and directorship information is sourced from Companies House public records.

Part 7

What This Response Asks of Defra

Request 1

Update the Fuel Authorisation Test

Replace BS 3841:1994 as the sole basis for authorising fuels. Any fuel authorised for SCA use should demonstrate compliance with PM₂.₅, CO, NOx, and OGC limits in mg/m³ at a defined reference oxygen level.

Request 2

Address the Open Fireplace Loophole

The consultation should explicitly address whether authorised fuels should continue to be permitted in open fireplaces in SCAs. If yes, Defra should publish the PM₂.₅, CO, NOx, and OGC emission profile of authorised smokeless fuels when burned in open fires under real-world conditions.

Request 3

Harmonise Measurement Units

Adopt mg/m³ at 13% O₂ as the standard unit for all emission limits — appliances and fuels. The continued use of g/h for some regimes and mg/m³ for others prevents meaningful comparison.

Request 4

Mandate PM₂.₅ Data on Fuel Packaging

Every manufactured solid fuel should carry tested PM₂.₅ emission data in mg/m³, not just a generic health statement.

Request 5

Strengthen Health Labelling

The WHO states there is no safe level of PM₂.₅ exposure (WHO Global Air Quality Guidelines, 2021). The label should name specific health conditions and state that indoor burning is a significant source of household PM₂.₅ exposure.

Request 6

Ensure Independent Testing Governance

The body responsible for fuel authorisation and appliance exemption should be structurally independent of the solid fuel industry. The Defra Smoke Control Area website should be transferred to a body with no financial relationship with the regulated industry.

Request 7

Commission an Independent Comparison Study

Defra should commission testing of all currently authorised smokeless fuels for PM₂.₅ (mg/m³), CO, NOx, and OGC, using Ecodesign-equivalent methodology, in a range of appliance types including open fireplaces.

Request 8

Learn from Ireland

Study and adopt Ireland's fuel-level intervention model. Dublin's coal ban produced a 70% reduction in black smoke and a 5.7% reduction in mortality. The UK's 2026 consultation addresses only the appliance. The Irish evidence demonstrates that fuel-level regulation produces health benefits that appliance-level regulation alone cannot deliver.

Two Complementary Arguments, One Deadline

This response and the accompanying Technical Evidence Dossier present two complementary cases to Defra. The Dossier demonstrates that the UK has no regulatory pathway for masonry heaters — the cleanest solid fuel technology available — and proposes BS EN 15544 recognition. This response demonstrates that the testing framework itself is internally contradictory, with three incompatible regimes leaving critical gaps in fuel regulation, open fireplace use, and measurement comparability.

Both responses share the same deadline. Both share the same goal: a testing and regulatory framework that accurately measures what matters — PM₂.₅, CO, NOx, OGC, and efficiency — in comparable units, for all fuels and all appliances.
